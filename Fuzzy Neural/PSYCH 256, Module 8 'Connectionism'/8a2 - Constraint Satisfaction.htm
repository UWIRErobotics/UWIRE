8a2: Connectionism: Constraint Satisfaction

So far, the neuron doctrine amounts to two claims:

  1. The brain is composed of many neurons interconnected in a complex way.
  2. The activity of individual neurons gives rise to mental phenomena and/or behavior.

Up to the 20th century, research seems to compel acceptance of the first claim. However, merely knowing about neurons and that there are a lot of the them arranged somehow in the brain does not provide any answer to the second question. Behaviorist psychologists, e.g., James Watson, as you may recall, said that the brain worked like a telephone network. However, that claim is not very enlightening: The way in which the behavior of individual neurons gives rise to the performance of the whole brain is still a mystery. A crucial idea in this research was provided by the Gestalt psychologists, who proposed that cognition is a kind of optimization. 

Koffka (1935) claimed that our minds work by attempting to satisfy a set of goals to the maximum extent possible.

Consider the following figure:

Probably what you perceive is two lines intersecting (or one line crossing above another line). Why do you not see four lines meeting at a point? Koffka argued that we see the picture the way we do because we tend to interpret scenes in a way that maximizes overall simplicity. That is, we have a constraint that compels us to interpret scenes in a way that involves the fewest number of objects possible. Put another way, our perception of the scene is optimal in the sense that it satisfies the constraint of simplicity to the greatest possible extent.

In the 1940s, researchers explored the idea that a network of neuron-like individuals might work by means of optimizing the satisfaction of constraints. This idea amounts to the following claims:

  1. The activity of neurons constrains the activity of neurons to which they are connected.
  2. The whole network functions to optimize the satisfaction of these constraints.

McCulloch & Pitts (1943) showed that a simple network of interacting units could, in principle, compute the functions of predicate logic (e.g., p and q, p or q, and so on). Hebb (1949) proposed a mathematical model of neurons (called the cell assembly theory) that showed how a network of simple neurons could learn things by changing the constraints that they placed on each other.

Hebb’s theory provides at least an initial answer to the second claim of the neuron doctrine above, namely how it is that the activity of individual neurons in a network can give rise to coherent activity in the network as a whole: The activity of each neuron constrains the behavior of the others to which it is connected so that the whole network (or assembly) has the same computational power as a digital computer. Hebb put the matter this way:

“The problem of understanding behaviour is the problem of understanding the total action of the nervous system and vice versa”

At this point, we face the very real prospect that the study of neural networks can lead to a general theory of thinking and intelligence to rival that of classic Cognitive Science.
