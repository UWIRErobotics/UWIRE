8b12: Connectionism: Neurological Plausibility

As ANNs were inspired by properties of the brain, it is instructive to review how brain-like ANNs really are.

First, the positive points. To begin with, knowledge in ANNs is distributed. That is, no one node in an ANN stands for any given symbol or concept. Instead, nodes participate in groups in the representation of concepts, with each node participating in several groups. This property is much like the population-level representation of knowledge that we see in really neuronal groups.

Also, ANNs display graceful degradation. If you begin to remove nodes from the hidden layer of an ANN, its performance degrades (that is, the error rate in classifying inputs) slowly. As Lashley observed in rats and mice, degradation of performance is proportional to the amount of damage inflected.

Finally, ANNs do a good job of generalizing from a few examples, as real brains do.

Now, the negative points. To start with, ANNs are very small compared with real brains. A human brain has billions of neurons whereas an ANN rarely has more than 1000 nodes. Experience shows that ANNs with much more than 1000 nodes simply cannot learn to make the distinctions that they are required to make in a reasonable amount of time. Obviously, real human brains do not have this problem. This problem is reminiscent of the scaling problem for rule-based systems. It is not clear that the neural net techniques that have been developed so far will scale up in the sense that simply adding more neurons will not necessarily make an ANN smarter or more capable. Of course, there may be answers to this problem. Perhaps a different kind of ANN will overcome it. Alternatively, it may be that ANNs must be specially structured before they can get much larger. This move seems like a natural one too, in view of the fact that neuroscientific research indicates that the brain is highly structured into separate regions and is not simply a huge mass of homogenously connected neurons.

Of course, the connectionist paradigm has been founded on many simplifying assumptions. Modelers concentrated on modeling the firing rate of neurons, for example, and omitted the information that might be carried by, say, the pattern of neural firing or the identify of the neurotransmitters involved in firing. We will return to these matters in the lecture on neuroscience.

Finally, the backpropagation procedure itself has no obvious neurological correlate. As Churchland and Sejnowski point out, a neuron does not send activation one way along a dendrite and then conduct error signals back along the same route. However, it may be that such signals exist in the form of feedback dendrites from other neurons.
