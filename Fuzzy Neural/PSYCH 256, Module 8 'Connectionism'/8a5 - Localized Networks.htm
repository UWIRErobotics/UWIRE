8a5: Connectionism: Localized Networks

One way of harnessing a network to simulate cognitive work is to assign symbolic interpretations to the nodes and assign significance to their activation levels. Consider the very simply localized network in the figure below. 

In this network, every node represents a symbol. The three nodes at the bottom each represent a letter of the alphabet, “c”, “a”, and “t”. The top two nodes each represent an English word, namely “cat” and “rat”. The links depicted with solid lines each represent an excitatory link whereas links with dashed lines represent inhibitory links. On the whole, this network could represent the cognitive task of recognizing which word is being presented (“cat” or “rat”) by virtue of the letters that the presentation contains (“c”, “a”, and “t”).

In this network, each letter that is part of a word tends to increase the activation of the node that represents that word. Each letter that is not part of a word tends to suppress or decrease the activation of the node that represents the word. Furthermore, each word node tends to deactivate the other word node. Each link, then, acts as a constraint on the activation of the nodes involved. An excitatory link will tend to constrain two nodes to be active together whereas an inhibitory link will tend to constrain two nodes to never be active together.

When this network is “switched on” or assigned non-zero activation levels, what happens in effect is that each node cooperates with its friends (that are linked by excitatory links) and competes with its enemies (that are linked by inhibitory links) in order to maintain activation for themselves. In the example above, the nodes representing the words “cat” and “rat” compete with each other to gain the activation coming from the nodes representing the letters “c”, “a”, and “t”. 

Over time, each node will change activation according to the rules discussed above. That is, activation will spread throughout the network in such a way that the current activation level of every node, together with the activation function of every node, will determine the next level of activation of every node. With luck, after the activation has been spreading through the network for some time, the activation level of the nodes will cease to change very much from one time to the next time. In other words, the network will settle into a steady state. In this stead state, some nodes will probably be active and some will have been rendered inactive. In a network like the one above, we can interpret the activation level of the nodes to mean that the node does or does not fit the situation presented.

After the network above has settled, probably the “cat” node will remain active whereas the “rat” node will have been rendered inactive. This result can be interpreted to mean that the presentation of the letters “c”, “a”, and “t” fit with the word “cat” and not with the word “rat”. In other words, the initial presentation says “cat” not “rat”. 

A network like this can tell us what the best interpretation of its inputs is given the constraints that apply to its set of representations.

The figure above displays possible outcomes of spreading activation. The horizontal axis represents time whereas the vertical axis represents the magnitude of change in activation throughout the network at any given time. The solid curve depicts the evolution of a network that experiences large changes initially but then settles into a stable state. The dashed curve depicts the evolution of a network in which the magnitude of overall activation change oscillates without settling.
