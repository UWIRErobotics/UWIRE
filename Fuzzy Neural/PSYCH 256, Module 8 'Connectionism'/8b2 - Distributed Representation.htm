8b2: Connectionism: Distributed Representation

In a localized network, each node stands for a single concept such as “dog” or “cat”. Research in neuroscience has indicated that this claim is not true of the human brain. The way this point is often put is that there is no “grandmother neuron”, that is, a neuron in your brain that fires if (and only if) you think about your grandmother. Furthermore, as Karl Lashley (1890–1958) discovered (ca. 1935), the cognitive deficit (or damage) that results from the removal of neurons from the brain is in proportion to the number of neurons lost. So, the removal of a few neurons has no appreciable affect on a person’s cognitive capacity, e.g., their ability to identify their grandmother is left intact. However, removal of a large number of neurons causes large-scale cognitive problems, such as the loss of the ability to speak.

Instead of each neuron representing a single concept, neurons work in groups to represent things.

There is a group of neurons in the visual cortex that keep firing when there is an object moving upwards in your field of view. When there is no such object, the neurons fire very little and somewhat randomly. This neuronal group represents the perception that “something is moving upwards here”.

We can tell that this set of neurons forms a group because each neuron is heavily connected to the others in the group, and more sparsely connected to neurons outside the group.

Also, as you might expect, this group of neurons tends to suppress the activation of another group of neurons that represents the fact that “something is moving downwards here.” So, we see that neuronal groups, not individual neurons per se, have excitatory and inhibitory links to other groups.

Finally, neurons within a group such as those just mentioned behave similarly but not identically. 

In the presence of an upward-moving object, most neurons in that group will vigorous whereas some will fire only somewhat vigorously. A few will raise their activation level very little. However, in the presence of an object moving upwards but also a little sideways, some of those that fired vigorously before will fire less vigorously, whereas some that fired lazily before will begin to fire more vigorously now.

As a result, it is really the average behavior of the group of neurons that represents knowledge, e.g., “something is moving upwards” instead of the behavior of an individual neuron. Another way of putting this claim is to say that the representation of concepts is distributed across the group of neurons instead of being localized at any one node.
