8b1: Connectionism: Overview

As we saw in the previous lecture, localized networks are somewhat brain-like in their mode of operation. We can summarize their brain-like qualities in the following points:

  1. They consist of a network of nodes.
  2. They operate in parallel.
  3. They perform cognitive tasks holistically.

    The last point refers to the fact that the outcome produced by a localized network is the product of the action of the whole network. That is, the solution the network settles on (assuming that it settles at all) is the state that satisfies all the constraints in the network to the greatest degree possible.

    However, as we also say, localized networks are un-brain-like in some significant ways. For example, the nodes of localized networks are transparent, that is, they each correspond to a specific symbol or concept. Also, localized networks are not effective at learning new concepts. Research in neuroscience indicates that neither feature is true of neuronal groups in the brain: No one neuron seems to correspond to a given concept, and brains are excellent at learning new concepts.

    Following the work of researchers like Hebb, some cognitive scientists pursued what have come to be called distributed connectionist networks or artificial neural networks (ANNs). ANNs are designed to be brain-like insofar as they are not transparent and they are good at learning. In this lecture, we will discuss distributed connectionist networks and how they learn things. We will also assess their strengths and weaknesses as models of human intelligence and cognition.
