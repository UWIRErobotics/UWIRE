8b6: Connectionism: Gradient Descent

Recall from our earlier discussion that neural networks (localized or distributed) work by performing some kind of optimization. A feedforward network trained using backpropagation is also performing a kind of optimization. In this case, it is minimizing the overall amount of error in the network. 

Recall that the backpropagation principle works by calculating the amount of error in the weight of each connection in the network. Imagine a space (call it the error space) in which the weight of each node is a dimension of the space. Another dimension constitutes the percent error, that is the percentage of misclassifications that the network makes at a given setting of connection weights. The percent error starts out pretty high at the beginning of training. The object of training is to reduce the percent error as much as is feasible.

In terms of the weight space, this means that the network starts out at a point in the error space that denotes a large amount of error. As training continues, weights are adjusted so that the network moves towards the point of zero percent error. Of course, some weight adjustments actually increase the error somewhat and the overall error will never actually reach zero, but it should get close if the network is well configured and you are a little fortunate. This process of moving from a high amount of error to a low amount is known as gradient descent and is depicted in the figure below. 

Gradient descent simply refers to the adjustment of connection weights so that the network makes as few mistakes as possible under the circumstances. Optimization in this case amounts to weight adjustments to minimize error.
