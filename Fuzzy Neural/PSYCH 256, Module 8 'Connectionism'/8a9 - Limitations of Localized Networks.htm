8a9: Connectionism: Limitations of Localized Networks

Localized networks also have a few limitations that should be noted.

Firstly, localized networks do not support learning very well. Note that none of the systems that we have discussed, DECO and ECHO, can learn new concepts or establish new links among them. There have been some notable exceptions to this generalization.

Copycat (Hofstadter) is an analogy making program that learns by making and testing hypotheses about the structure of letter-string analogies (abc:xyz :: abd: ??). Copycat makes hypotheses about whether the successor or predecessor relation is being applied in the source analog.

Humans sometimes have to be more creative in their problem solving, such as positing new concepts.

In the process of considering what to do after graduation, you may actually learn what your goals and priorities really are.

In addition, localized networks generally assume that all the major constraints are known at the outset. Constraints are another thing that can emerge as part of the process of problem solving.

Local representations can represent some, but not all, logical relationships.

  1. and (p & q) = symmetric excitatory link;
  2. not-both ~(p & q) = symmetry inhibitory link;
  3. if-then (p → q) = asymmetric excitatory link
  4. or (p v q) = ??

Thus, some knowledge is hard to capture straightforwardly using localized networks.

Finally, although localized networks work in parallel instead of serially, they are not very brain-like in other respects. There is no evidence that individual neurons in our brains represent individual concepts.

There is no neuron in your brain that represents your grandmother.

Some populations of neurons do seem to represent particular sorts of information, such as whether you looking at a familiar face, but they represent things in a distributed and not localized manner. We will discuss connectionist networks using distributed representations in the next half of the module.
