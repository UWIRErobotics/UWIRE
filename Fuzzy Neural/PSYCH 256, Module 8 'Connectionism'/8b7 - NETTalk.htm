8b7: Connectionism: NETTalk

Above all others, NETTalk was the “killer app” of early neural networks. That is, NETTalk was the neural network whose performance really got many people excited and talking about what neural networks can accomplish.

The problem addressed by NETTalk was turning text into speech. The problem basically involves mapping a text (sequence of words) into phonemes, symbols capturing what sounds the vocal tract can produce in a given language.

“cancer” becomes /kænsr/, “lead” can be either /led/ or /li:d/.

DEC has spent 20 years developing an expert system known as DECtalk to address this problem using rules. The early versions in the 1980s correctly mapped about 78% of the test texts. Twenty years later, the accuracy is about 95%.

In 1987, Sejnowski and Rosenberg created NETtalk to perform the same function. After just 50 training cycles, the network performed with about 98% accuracy on its training text. On a separate test text, NETtalk performed with about 78% accuracy, much like DECtalk at the time.

NETtalk proved to many cognitive scientists that ANNs were capable of learning to perform tasks at a level comparable to that of rule-based systems but with far less effort. Also, a tape released by the authors recording how NETtalk performed at intervals in its training sounds somewhat like the stages an infant goes through when learning to pronounce words. (There is an MP3 of this on the Salk Institute Website). NETtalk showed that such complex tasks as this one could really be learned simply by examples, somewhat like the way people learn.
