8a3: Connectionism: Simplified Neural Networks

You can see here that we face the issue of whether or not the study of the brain is compatible with the classical Cognitive Science paradigm. To address this issue, we first need to explore the neural account of thinking and intelligence as it has developed since the 1940s. To begin this exploration, we will look at what are called localized networks.

Any network consists of two sets of things:

  1. A set of nodes and
  2. A set of connections among nodes.

In the case of a brain, the nodes are neurons and the connections are called synapses. These can be seen as shown in this Synapses animation. 

However, connectionist researchers, until recently, have relied on a simplified model of a real neural network. It is this simplified model that we will discuss here.

First of all, let us consider the nodes. Now, a real neuron is a cell, with a body, a nucleus, and a complicated internal metabolism. The early connectionists, however, concentrated on one aspect of a neuron, its level of activity or, equivalently, its activation level. In a real neuron, the level of activity is its firing rate, that is, the number of times that the neuron sends signals to its neighbors through its synapses. In a node in an artificial neural network, the firing rate is often modeled as a number in the range [0…1], where a low number corresponds to a low firing rate and a high number corresponds to a high firing rate. 

The connections among nodes, i.e., the synapses, are modeled as links from node to node, as in the figure of a simple, one-way, one-to-one connection in the figure below.

In addition to the activation level of each unit, e.g., activation a1 of unit N1, there is a link from N1 to N2. Now, in a brain, a link or synapse linking one neuron to another has at least two features. 

  1. The link has a connection strength. A link with a strong connection means that the source neuron has a greater influence on the target neuron than would be the case with a weak connection strength.
  2. The link may be either excitatory or inhibitory. 
    1. An excitatory link is one that causes the target neuron to be more active as the source neuron becomes more active. When a neuron becomes very active, it tends to raise the activation level of other neurons to which it is connected via excitatory links.
    2. An inhibitory link is one that causes the target neuron to be less active as the source neuron becomes more active. When a neuron becomes very active, it tends to suppress the activation level of other neurons to which it is connected via inhibitory links.

In the case of an artificial neural network, the strength and excitatory or inhibitory nature of a connection are modeled with another number, namely the connection weight. A connection weight is often modeled as a number in the range [-1…1]. A connection weight of near 0 magnitude represents a weak connection, whereas a connection weight of near 1 magnitude represents a strong connection.

Now, there is a very simple way of modeling the influence that one node, N1, has on another node, N2. The signal that N1 gives to N2 is simply the activation level of N1 multiplied by the connection weight of the link. (See the figure above.) There are three kinds of effects that nodes can have on each other as a result of this setup.

  1. If a1 = 0, then a1 • w1,2 = 0, meaning that N1 exerts no influence on N2.
  2. If a1 > 0, then a1 • w1,2 will have the same sign as the weight w1,2.
    1. If w1,2 > 0, then the input to N2 is positive, that is, excitatory.
    2. If w1,2 < 0, then the input to N2 is negative, that is, inhibitory.

So, a node in a simplified network operates by influencing others nodes to which it is connected by either raising or suppressing their activation levels, in proportion to its own activation level.
