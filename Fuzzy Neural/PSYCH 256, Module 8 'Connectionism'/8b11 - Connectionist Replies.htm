8b11: Connectionism: Connectionist Replies

The essential connectionist reply to the problem of systematicity is to argue that not all ANNs suffer from it. One approach to this problem is to make the representational scheme more complex so that it learns and preserves not only superficial information about its inputs but structural information about them as well. Such schemes include tensor product encodings (Smolensky), holographically reduced representations (Plate), recursive autoassociative memory (Pollack 1988). The mathematical details fall outside the scope of this lecture but suffice it to say that there are very many ways of using nodes and weights to represent information, some of which are sensitive to systematic information.

Besides changing the encoding scheme used in a network, another possibility is to change a network’s structure. One such move comes in the form of a recurrent network. Elman (1991) added an extra hidden layer to a standard feedforward network that stores the state of the network’s hidden layer when an expected input is presented.

Many sentences consist of a subject, verb, and then object, as in “The cat ate the food.” However, this scheme may be thwarted if a sentence is included inside another sentence, as in “The cat the man brought home ate the food.”

Elman trained a network to classify subject-verb-object word sequences as sentences. When going through a sentence, the network tries to predict what kind of word will come next. After “The cat …”, the network predicts a verb, e.g,. “ate”. However, if it finds another subject, it places the state of the hidden layer resulting from “The cat” in storage in its extra layer and parses “the man brought home” until it finds a complete sentence. At that point, it recalls the activation state stored previously and continues on through “…ate the food.” A recurrent network is depicted in the figure below.

This move is a very curious one. On a rule-based view, both sentences are to be approached the same way, namely by applying the rules of grammar in light of the knowledge of grammar and vocabulary. The difference in sentences merely calls for the application of different rules but the procedure is essentially the same in both cases.

In Elman’s recurrent network, however, the sentences “The cat ate the food” and “The cat the man brought home ate the food” call for different procedures. The second sentence is parsed by invoking the extra mechanism of the context layer, whereas the first sentence is parsed without invoking this procedure.

Is this difference significant? Well, on the rule-based view, your knowledge of sentences is represented explicitly in the form of rules of grammar and vocabulary. On Elman’s view, some of your knowledge of language is represented explicitly in the form of connection weights, as you would expect. However, some of your knowledge of language resides not in one or another set of connection weights but implicitly in structure of the network itself. 

Imagine that you are able to search through your store of knowledge (by some means) and write down everything found there. If Elman is right, that list would not include things like the possibility of embedding sentences within sentences. That is not something you know, as such; it something you are simply able to do.
