8b8: Connectionism: ALVINN

Pomerleau (1993) developed the Autonomous Land Vehicle In a Neural Network (ALVINN), an ANN that can drive cars and other light vehicles. ALVINN works by mapping a video image of the road ahead into a driving direction, e.g., “go straight”, “turn left a little”, etc. Training data was collected by recording video image/driving direction pairs from a human driving the vehicle. ALVINN was then trained on the data (10 minutes with backpropagation) and was ready to drive the same vehicle. ALVINN has driven at speeds of up to 70 mph for up to 90 miles on public highways around Pittsburgh.

An ANN is very appropriate for this domain because it can learn from examples. An obvious alternative, an expert system, will not work because there is no theory of driving upon which to base the rules. Furthermore, driving is a “noisy” kind of task, for which many different cues may (or may not) be relevant in a given situation. This tolerance for noise is a general feature of ANNs: that they can perform even in unfamiliar situations, unlike rule-based systems.

One limitation of ALVINN is that it addresses only one part of driving, namely lane following. Deciding when to get on or off the road, change lanes, etc. is quite beyond it. Pomerleau has been working on integrating ALVINN with a rule-based system to address these more challenging aspects of driving.
