8a1: Connectionsim: Overview

The classic Cognitive Science paradigm is inspired by the analogy of thinking with computation. On this view, thinking is the result of the manipulation of mental representations by mental procedures. As we have seen, there are tensions within the paradigm itself about the kinds of mental representations and procedures that best account for the phenomena of interest, namely intelligence and cognition. Here are, roughly speaking, the possibilities that we have discussed so far:

  1. Rules and search: Intelligence is the result of knowledge, in the form of rules, combined by search through knowledge space.
  2. Concepts and association: Intelligence is the application of knowledge, in the form of concepts, applied through association and matching.
  3. Images and imagery: Some intelligence involves the use of visual mental images as representations and the abilities of the mind’s eye as procedures.

The diversity among this group shows that the thinking/computation paradigm is not a conceptual monolith but rather a model to guide the formulation of theories of cognition. 

One of the limitations of the thinking/computation model is that it does not extend to the hardware involved. That is, the digital computer, which performs the computing, is nothing much like a human brain, which is what seems to perform the labor involved in human cognition. Turing, you may recall, thought that the difference between brain and computer is of no consequence: After all, he argued, intelligence is strictly a matter of software. So, the details of hardware are not relevant in the end. However, the difficulties encountered in deriving an overall account of intelligence and cognition on the basis of this assumption have convinced many that it simply does not hold up. 

One obvious alternative source of inspiration for theories of intelligence and cognition is the study of the hardware involved, namely the brain. Studies of the brain have revealed that the brain is hugely complicated network of 10 to 100 billion tiny individual cells now called neurons. Curiously, no individual neuron seems to correspond to anything like a symbol in the class Cognitive Science paradigm. Also, the way in which neurons work does not bear much resemblance to the way that symbols participate in computations. Neurons seem to work by modifying or constraining the behavior of other neurons through their connections. In addition, instead of performing computations in a one-at-a-time or serial manner, neurons in the brain operate all-at-once or in parallel.

The paradigm of cognitive modeling inspired by studies of the brain is usually known as connectionism because of the densely interconnected nature of neurons in the brain. Connectionist models of cognition seek to imitate the workings of the brain by employing neuron-like units as the basic form of representation and a process of constraint satisfaction as the way in which the units are manipulated. See the table below.
