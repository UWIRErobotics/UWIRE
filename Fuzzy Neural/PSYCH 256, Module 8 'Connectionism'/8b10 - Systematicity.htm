8b10: Connectionism: Systematicity

One of the features of distributed representations is that the concepts acquired by a network are organized associatively; that is, by similarity. As a result, similar inputs to a trained network tend to produce similar outputs. In some respects, this feature is a good thing. It means that the network can deal intelligently with inputs that it has never seen before. Indeed, this feature is a central measure of success of distributed networks: After learning to classify a set of training inputs successfully, the test is to see how the network performs on a new set of data.

When a network is exposed to inputs that it has not seen before, it tends to classify them in the same category as similar inputs that were in its training set. In effect, the network generalizes from the examples to a whole space of inputs. This behavior seems to capture an important aspect of human intelligence, namely the ability to generalize spontaneously from a few examples. It is this ability that distributed networks seem to capture best.

However, the feature that allows ANNs to generalize so easily can also result in some apparently severe limitations on what a distributed network can learn. Fodor and Pylyshyn (1988), for example, argued that distributed networks fail to display systematicity. Human thinking, they argue, displays a kind of systematic character that the functioning of a feedforward network does not display.

To see this point, consider the following sentences:

  1. Bill likes Mary.
  2. Mary likes Bill.

    These sentences are very similar; indeed each consists of exactly the same words. The only superficially obvious difference is a small one of word order. Because these sentences are so similar, it would be very difficult to train a feedforward network to classify them differently (provided the network is more general in purpose than making that one distinction). 

    However, you know that the sentences are very different in a deeper sense. That is, they mean very different things. A rule-based approach provides a very natural account of the different. There are units of knowledge, words in this case, that can be sequenced in different ways by rules to form different combinations. Recall Newell’s argument that intelligence consists in the ability to combine chunks of knowledge in different ways as the situation demands. Fodor and Pylyshyn insist that the ability to be intelligent in this way is a general feature of human thinking and call it systematicity. Since feedforward networks fail to display sysetmaticity, then they cannot be good models of human cognition or intelligence.
