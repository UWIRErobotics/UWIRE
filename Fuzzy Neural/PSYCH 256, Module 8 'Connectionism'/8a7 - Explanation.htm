8a7: Connectionism: Explanation

A similar account of explanation can be given using localized networks. When we explain something, we generally seek out the explanation that gives the best account of the available evidence, plus whatever else we know about the given situation. Again, it is fairly straightforward to model this sort of inference with a network of constraints.

The ECHO (Explanatory COherence) model of Thagard provides such a model. In ECHO, nodes represent either evidence or hypotheses. (Also, there is a special node, data priority, which serves to activate the immediate evidence in the given situation.) There are a couple of excitatory links, namely explanation and analogy. An explanation link exists between evidence and each hypothesis that would explain that evidence. Also, an explanatory link exists between hypotheses that explain the same piece of evidence. An analogy link exists between similar hypotheses that explain similar evidence. (As we know, analogy is a complex relationship, but it is reduced to a simple one here for the purpose of convenience.)

There are two kinds of inhibitory link in ECHO, namely contradiction and competition. A contradiction link exists between two pieces of evidence that are incompatible with each other, i.e., they cannot both be true of the same situation. A competition link exists between hypotheses that both explain a certain piece of evidence but are not compatible with each other.

Suppose you expect to meet your friend Fred at the cafeteria but he does not show up. You find out from a mutual friend that Fred was in the library recently. Probably, you would try to reach a good explanation for this evidence, in light of your expectations and your general knowledge about your friend.

This network represents the constraints on forming the best explanation (from two candidates) of the observed evidence. It includes both the observed evidence, “Fred was in the library” and “Fred did not come to the cafeteria”, and further knowledge about Fred, namely that he wants high grades and likes to party. This network shows how hypotheses like “Fred is studying” sit in relations to further hypotheses, about Fred’s motivations (“Fred wants high grades”) and his interests (“Fred likes to party”). If we feed activation into the network via the EVIDENCE node and allow it to settle, the active nodes will correspond to the best hypothesis among the available possibilities.
