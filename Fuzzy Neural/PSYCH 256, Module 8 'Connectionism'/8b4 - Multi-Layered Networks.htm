8b4: Connectionism: Multi-Layered Networks

In their (1969) book, Minsky and Papert proved that perceptrons could not learn simple logical functions such as XOR via the PLP. They also speculated that there is no learning procedure that would allow a network to learn to compute any given function. There might be ways of teaching networks to compute functions such as XOR, but you would have no guarantee that they would learn perform correctly for all inputs. Such was the authority of Minsky and Papert that their speculation essentially squashed research into neural networks until the 1980s.

By an interesting coincidence, Bryson and Ho (1969) had just invented a procedure, known as backpropagation, that could teach a multilayered network to learn to compute functions such as XOR. However, neither they nor other researchers were able to prove that this procedure was guaranteed to produce correct performance for all inputs. In fact, it turns out that the backpropagation procedure is not capable of producing a correct performance for all inputs. For this reason, combined with Minsky and Papert’s influence, the backpropagation procedure went essentially unnoticed until the 1980s.

In the early 1980s, however, Rumelhart, McClelland, Smolensky, et al. began to play around with backpropagation and discovered that it was able to simulate some impressive cognitive performances such as converting a text into speech. This sort of ability, even without an absolute guarantee of success, stimulated great interest in multilayered networks.
